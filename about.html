About

A modern, browser-based remake of Laser Squad — Julian Gollop's 1988 turn-based tactics game, the one that taught X-COM everything it knew.

This is built entirely in conversation with Claude: I describe what I want, it does the typing. The aim isn't a perfect port. It's to capture the feeling of the original — the action-point economy where every step and shot is a decision, the line-of-sight tension, the way a turn ends with you holding your breath — in something that looks clean on a modern screen.

Two players, hot-seat, on one screen for now. No build tools, no frameworks: plain JavaScript and hand-drawn SVG, so the whole thing is a folder of files you could read end to end. A computer opponent comes later.

Development diary

4 JUNE 2026

Standing in doorways

A snag from the doors work: you couldn't stop in a doorway, because clicking an open door closed it. Now an open doorway is a normal tile you can step into and hold — useful for peeking and firing from a threshold. Closing moved to a small ✕ badge that appears on an open door next to your soldier, so the two actions never fight each other.

4 JUNE 2026 MILESTONE

Doors and windows

The compound is sealed now. Doors start closed — they block movement and sight, and you spend action points to open or shut one you're standing next to. So the level became a proper breach: the attackers have to crack the building open, and a defender left watching a doorway can fire on whoever comes through it.

Windows are the more interesting half. You can see through glass but not shoot through it — so you might spot a defender through a window and still have no shot, until you break the glass (smash it from the next tile, or put a round into it from range; either way the first shot just shatters it and stops). A broken window becomes a firing port. The neat consequence: a soldier behind intact glass can watch you cross the yard but can't open fire until the pane goes. Sight and line-of-fire are now two different things, which is exactly the kind of texture that made the original sing.

4 JUNE 2026

Seeing the shot

Until now a shot was just a line in the combat log, which made firefights hard to follow — you couldn't always tell one had happened. Now every shot, yours or the enemy's, throws a bolt from shooter to target with a muzzle flash, an impact ring and the damage floating up off whoever's hit, or the round sailing wide on a miss. There's optional sound too — off by default, a toggle up top — synthesised in the browser, so there's nothing to download. A small change that makes the game read like a firefight rather than a spreadsheet.

3 JUNE 2026 MILESTONE

Fog of war

Now you only see what your soldiers can see. The map's there, but enemies are hidden until one of your squad gets eyes on them — and the moment you lose sight, they leave a faded "?" where you last saw them, so you can reason about where they've gone rather than having the information snatched away. Walk a soldier forward and the dark peels back; the first time an unseen sentry opens up on you from a doorway you hadn't cleared, the whole game tightens.

Because it's two players on one screen, there's now a "pass the device" card between turns, so nobody sees the other's view. And the danger warning finally makes sense: reachable tiles get a red outline when an enemy you can actually see could shoot you there — never a hint about the ones you can't, which would be cheating. Knowing where you're safe to step, based only on what you've scouted, is the whole game.

3 JUNE 2026

Reading the battlefield

A legibility pass on the overlays. Movement and field-of-view were both wearing blue, which was muddling two different ideas, so a soldier's view is now green, enemy danger is red and layered on top so overlaps read as threat rather than mud. And the move-field comes in two tones: solid where you can go and still bank the action points to fire on reaction next turn, grey where moving spends you out. You can now see, before you commit, exactly where you stop being a threat.

3 JUNE 2026 MILESTONE

Opportunity fire and cover

The heart of the game, and the reason you never spend your last action points. End a turn with a soldier facing the right way and some AP banked, and on the enemy's turn it'll fire on anyone who crosses its view. Movement is no longer instant — a soldier walks tile by tile and stops dead the moment it's spotted, having spent the AP for the steps it took. The first time an enemy gets caught edging through a doorway you'd left covered, the whole thing clicks.

The hard part isn't the shooting, it's making it legible — automatic reactions are notorious for feeling arbitrary. So: hover any soldier and the board paints the exact tiles it can watch, walls and arc included; when a reaction lands you see the tracer and the log says so. Walls now give cover too, cutting the hit chance when a target is tucked behind one — and the percentage you already see on hover simply drops, so the maths is never hidden. The facing ring, cosmetic until now, suddenly decides games.

3 JUNE 2026

Turning, properly

The first build let you pivot a soldier by clicking empty ground — except it only worked on tiles out of walking range, which is backwards and useless while you still have action points. Replaced it with a ring of eight arrows around the selected soldier: click one to turn on the spot. Movement still auto-faces the way you walked, so the ring is only for when you deliberately want to watch a different direction — which won't matter much until a soldier's facing decides what it can react to.

2 JUNE 2026 MILESTONE

The vertical slice

First playable build. One map — a compound raid: blue squad breaches from the west, red holds inside. The core loop is all here: select a soldier, see where its action points can carry it, click to move, click an enemy in your sights to fire. Line of sight is real — you can't shoot through walls, so taking a doorway means giving up cover. Hit chance falls off with range. Last squad standing wins.

Graphics are deliberately placeholder — coloured discs with a facing notch and a health bar. The point of this build was to prove the mechanics feel right before drawing a single nice pixel. Soldiers track which way they're facing, but the can't-see-behind-you rule doesn't bite yet; it arrives with opportunity fire, where it earns its keep.

Next: doors and grenades, then a proper art pass — and somewhere in there, an opponent that fights back.
